Fear of Inequality

For many Black pregnant women in this country, one extra burden is the intimidating statistics indicating the higher risks for Black women during childbirth. Black women have higher rates of first-time cesarean delivery and are 50% more likely to have a premature baby than white women.

In the face of those statistics, Quintett Bowers, like many Black women, feel like she needs an advocate by her side.

"With my doula, I feel like they're so much in tune to the mother and the baby," said Bowers. This is her fourth pregnancy but her first time hiring a doula. Her doula already visited her house, met all her family members and knew what type of music and massage she likes.

A lot of uncomfortableness involved in her previous childbirths. There were restrictions she wasn't aware of and birth plans that never got followed. She was watched by numerous medical interns while pushing the baby out. "I felt like I was an animal on display at the zoo. I felt like they were not 100% with me and I was just another patient that they had to deal with."

One time her white friends shared their laboring experiences with her: The doctor paid full attention, fulfilling all their needs. "Why is it so different for y'all when at the end of the day, all of us are females?" Bowers thought. Racial biases happen, sometimes in very nuanced ways that can be hard to prove and quantified solely based on personal experience.

Several studies have pointed out that medical professionals perceived Hispanic and Black women as having higher pain tolerances, leading to disparities in pain management that cannot be explained by less perceived pain.

It wasn't until that conversation that the word systematic racism struck her. As a Black American, she knew discrimination happened in various forms. But there were good days when she could enjoy her life without worrying about it.

"I'm black doesn't mean that I experience racism every day of my life … When something happens, you don't sit back and think about it as, wow, I just experienced systematic racism. You thought they were having a bad day. It was just a misconnect," said Bowers.

Bowers wants someone who looks like her, understands her and will advocate for her this time. "[My doula] is Black. That's how I feel safe. I feel like they have the same struggles I had. I feel she has my best interests at heart," said Bowers.

In light of COVID-19 and some publicized studies about racial disparities in maternal mortality, Dashanna Hanlon, a doula serving primarily people of color, saw an increase in people hiring a doula and asking for a home birth. "You would think if it's medical, there can't be racism. But there definitely is. Black women do get ignored. We don't get attended to as quickly when we need something."

Some medical teams embrace doulas as an integral part, some not so much. There were times when Hanlon felt that medical professionals were annoyed when she was there to support her clients. "We show up at the hospital. And they think, oh, no, they're going to want to argue. They're going to want to fight me. And that's not the case. I'm there to care for [my clients] and make sure that they have what they need."

With no extra budget to hire a doula, Olive Deavers, 31, whose due date is April 29, can only count on her vocal and opinionated husband.

Working for a public health organization as a nutritionist, her daily works involve inspecting and improving state materials in nutrition education, breastfeeding and maternal childhood health.

Deavers wondered, perhaps, with her Master’s degree, stable finances and good healthcare, there’s a higher likelihood that she could prevent the worst case scenario.

She looked up the maternity death rates in multiple hospitals but the data wasn’t available. During her first therapist meeting after getting pregnant, Deavers talked about those numbers and concerns, “I was like, I really can't do anything about this, you know?” Instead of a major hospital, Deavers and her husband chose a regional hospital with less patients and more personalized care. They familiarized with half of the physicians in the maternity team.

Things are getting better after we have learned and adapted to the new kind of “normalcy.” Hospital policies have matured. Front-line workers have been vaccinated. We got ourselves familiar with sewing masks and video chatting.

But some familiar problems remain: sharp inequalities in maternity deaths, unemployment rates, housing and care for expectant mothers. These existed long before the pandemic but were exacerbated by it. Murray traces her anxiety to the health system’s lack of support for pregnant women, Molly’s decision to seek an abortion was directly tied to economic fears, and confusing information about the vaccines’ safety is at the root of many of Deavers concerns.
